#### Plaintiff Opening

Council, the proposition is narrow and time‑barred: Before 2027‑01‑01 00:00 PST, did Polymarket launch an official fungible token and publicly communicate that launch via its own channels? NFTs, promos, tokenized points, and co‑branded/community tokens do not count.

Theory
Polymarket did launch such a token within the window and acknowledged it through official communications. The decisive questions are who launched what, how it was described by Polymarket itself, and when.

Elements the proponent must prove (all required)
1) Fungibility and transferability: An on‑chain, user‑transferable fungible asset (e.g., ERC‑20‑type), not an NFT, points, or internal balance.
2) Official launch by Polymarket: The issuer/launcher is Polymarket (as brand/operator) or its authorized deployer under its control.
3) Official communication: Polymarket publicly communicated the token’s launch through channels it controls (primary site, official blog, verified social/account feeds, product or policy pages). Third‑party press is insufficient standing alone.
4) Timing: Both launch and official communication occurred on or before 2027‑01‑01 00:00 PST (08:00 UTC). Ambiguous timestamps must be resolved to this bar.

What does not satisfy the proposition
- NFTs, loyalty/XP/points (even if “tokenized” but not openly transferable), promo vouchers, or speculative placeholders.
- Third‑party/partner/community tokens, even if integrated on the platform, without Polymarket holding them out as its own official token.

Disputed issues framed for decision
- Identity: Is there a specific fungible token Polymarket held out as its own official asset (name/ticker/contract)?
- Control/issuance: Do deployer/multisig/signers or commissioning records tie the contract to Polymarket authority?
- Official speech: Did Polymarket’s own channels describe or announce the token as launched?
- Temporal placement: Do reliable, dated artifacts place the launch and the official communication before the PST deadline?
- Exclusion fit: Does the asset fall into any excluded category or a co‑launch not attributable to Polymarket?

Proof that would satisfy preponderance
- Dated, official Polymarket announcement identifying the token as its own and stating or implying launch availability.
- On‑chain evidence linking the token’s deployer/owner to Polymarket (e.g., previously attested multisig, audited release addresses, or consistent corporate custody patterns).
- Product/legal/policy materials on official domains characterizing the asset as Polymarket’s token.
- Archival captures confirming the content and dates of official pages/posts; on‑chain timestamping corroborating deployment/mint.

Proof that would defeat the proposition
- Clear, dated, official denials that Polymarket launched any token in time.
- Conclusive classification of any cited asset as NFT, points, promo, or a partner/community token.
- No trace of official Polymarket communication acknowledging a token launch before the deadline.
- Mismatch between claimed identifiers and any asset Polymarket publicly embraced as its own.

Forward‑looking evidentiary plan
- Authenticate Polymarket’s official channels and affiliated entities for the period.
- Collect dated announcements and product/policy pages referencing any token.
- Resolve contract provenance to Polymarket‑controlled keys or commissioned deployers.
- Fix all times to PST with archival and on‑chain records.
- Rigorously screen for exclusions and co‑branding.

Requested finding
On the completed record, find that Polymarket launched an official fungible token and publicly communicated that launch before 2027‑01‑01 00:00 PST, under a preponderance standard.

#### Defendant Argument

Council,

Under a preponderance standard, the proponent must show it is more likely than not that, before January 1, 2027 at 00:00 PST (08:00 UTC), Polymarket (i) launched a fungible token that it officially released as its own, and (ii) publicly communicated that launch through official channels, with timing fixed to the window and exclusions not triggered. On this record, that burden is not met.

Core problem with the proof posture
The proponent’s case rests almost entirely on two counsel‑written summaries (PX‑1, PX‑2). The actual primary materials they reference (the Polymarket docs pages and the Polygonscan page) are not in the record; only excerpts and characterizations are. PX‑1 itself concedes “Remaining official‑source limitations”: the captures were made after arbitration; there are no server publication timestamps; no archive snapshots; and the explorer evidence is merely corroborative. Those concessions go to authenticity, attribution, and, above all, timing—the very elements the proposition turns on. With those gaps, the Council cannot fix what was said by Polymarket, when, and in what exact language.

Element‑by‑element rebuttal
1) Fungibility and transferability (not proven)
PX‑2 reports that a “pUSD” is a standard ERC‑20, transferable, 6 decimals, backed by USDC. But there is no contract code, ABI, or transfer‑restriction analysis in the record; no on‑chain event timeline; no proof that transfers are open and user‑controlled as opposed to protocol‑restricted. A counsel summary of an absent web page does not carry the preponderance standard on this element.

2) Official launch by Polymarket (not proven)
The theory that listing “pUSD — CollateralToken (proxy)” on a “Contracts” docs page shows Polymarket launched the token assumes what it needs to prove. The record provides no deployer/admin provenance tying the cited address to a Polymarket‑controlled multisig or previously attested custody. Platforms routinely document integrated assets; documentation is not the same as issuer control. Without on‑chain provenance or a primary Polymarket attestation that it issued and is responsible for the token’s contract, “official launch by Polymarket” remains unproven.

3) Official communication of the launch (not proven)
The proposition requires public communication by Polymarket of the token’s launch, not merely internal component documentation. PX‑1/PX‑2 quote a “changelog” and a “V2 migration guide,” but those primary pages are not in evidence with headers, timestamps, or archive captures. The quoted language itself is equivocal on timing: an April 17, 2026 entry “targets” a go‑live; an April 28 entry says “CLOB V2 is live” and “points to the migration,” which speaks to an exchange upgrade, not an unequivocal token launch announcement. No official blog post, press page, or in‑app/verified social announcement introducing “Polymarket USD (pUSD)” as launched appears in the record.

4) Timing to the bar (not proven)
Even if one credited the quoted docs, PX‑1 acknowledges there are no server timestamps or archive snapshots. There is no block‑time evidence for contract creation/mint, no first‑use transactions, and no PST‑converted timeline tied to primary artifacts. Without that, the Council cannot reliably place both launch and official communication before January 1, 2027 at 00:00 PST.

Exclusions and classification concerns (unresolved on this record)
PX‑2 characterizes pUSD as a collateral token “backed by USDC.” That description is consistent with an internal accounting/custody wrapper used to settle trades, which the market’s exclusions caution against (tokenized point/balance systems or similar non‑public assets). If transfers are permissioned or limited to protocol contracts, it would not satisfy the user‑transferable fungible‑asset element. The proponent offers no code, policy page, or independent analysis to dispel those possibilities.

Weight of the evidence
The only concrete on‑chain reference in the record is a token address recited in PX‑1/PX‑2, paired with a claim that an explorer page’s title reads “Polymarket: pUSD Token.” Explorer labels are editable and not authoritative for issuer attribution; and, again, the underlying page is not in evidence with a timestamp. Under preponderance, unverified summaries with explicit authenticity/timing caveats do not outweigh the absence of primary, time‑fixed proof.

What would satisfy the proposition (and is missing)
- Primary, dated Polymarket communications (docs/blog/announcements or in‑app notices) stating that Polymarket launched “Polymarket USD (pUSD),” preserved with archive timestamps predating the bar.
- On‑chain provenance that the pUSD contract was deployed/administered by a Polymarket‑controlled address, with block‑time timestamps converted to PST.
- Technical confirmation of open ERC‑20 transferability (no protocol‑only transfer restrictions) and that the asset is not merely an internal points/balance wrapper.

Requested finding
On this closed record, the proponent has not shown—by a preponderance of the evidence—that before January 1, 2027 at 00:00 PST Polymarket launched an official fungible token and publicly communicated that launch via its own channels. Find for the defendant.

### Surrebuttals

#### Defendant Surrebuttal

Council,

The rebuttal purports to cure three defects with a “technical report” that repeats the same docs pages and adds an explorer label. On this closed record, those defects remain and preponderance is still not met.

1) Timing/authentication gap persists
• PX‑1 itself concedes the key limitation: the captures were made after the run and there are no server publication timestamps or archive snapshots. That was the core problem we identified, and nothing in the rebuttal changes it. The “technical report” cites what the pages read today and references internal view ids, but it does not add independent, time‑fixed evidence (e.g., Memento/Wayback, server‑signed headers, repo commits) showing those statements were publicly communicated on April 17/28, 2026 rather than later edits. Without time‑fixing, the Council cannot reliably place the alleged “official communication” before January 1, 2027 at 00:00 PST. The UTC→PST conversion is undisputed; what’s missing is proof that those entries existed on those dates.

2) “Official launch by Polymarket” is still not established
• The Contracts page excerpt in PX‑1/PX‑2 describes a “single source of truth for addresses used across the platform” and lists “pUSD — CollateralToken (proxy).” That is evidence of integration and address discovery, not issuer provenance or launch. There is no owner/admin mapping to a Polymarket‑controlled multisig, no deployer analysis, and no primary Polymarket statement that it issued the token. The rebuttal leans on a “Polygonscan: Contract Creator — Polymarket: Deployer 1” label cited only in the technical report narrative; explorer labels are editable and, without on‑chain key mapping in evidence, cannot carry issuer control under a preponderance standard.

3) Fungibility/transferability and exclusions remain unresolved
• The concept‑page bullet “Transferable — Yes” is a product claim, not technical proof. The record lacks code, policy, or transaction analysis showing open, user‑initiated EOA↔EOA transfers without protocol permissioning. PX‑1’s mention of “many recent token transactions” does not distinguish contract churn from free user transfers. If pUSD functions as an internal settlement wrapper “backed by USDC,” it risks falling within the spirit of excluded tokenized balance/points systems or, at minimum, fails the element requiring a user‑transferable fungible asset proven on this record.

4) “Official communication of the launch” is still ambiguous
• Even crediting the quoted docs, the April 17 entry “targets” a go‑live and the April 28 entry says “CLOB V2 is live” and points to migration. That language documents a system upgrade; it is not the same as a dated, unambiguous announcement that “Polymarket launched its token (Polymarket USD, pUSD) today,” and the record provides no contemporaneous blog, press, in‑app notice, or social post doing so. On a narrow proposition that requires public communication of a token launch, that ambiguity matters.

Bottom line
The plaintiff’s best evidence remains (a) mutable docs pages without independent, pre‑deadline time‑fixing and (b) a contracts roster proving integration, not issuance. Against that backdrop, the elements of (i) official launch by Polymarket and (ii) timely, official communication are not shown to be more likely than not. Find for the defendant.

#### Defendant Closing

Council,

Under a preponderance standard, the plaintiff must prove it is more likely than not that, before January 1, 2027 at 00:00 PST, Polymarket (i) launched an official fungible token and (ii) publicly communicated that launch via its own channels. On this record, key elements remain unproven.

Decisive gaps
1) Timing and authentication are not established. The plaintiff’s own exhibit (PX‑1) concedes the core limitation: the cited Polymarket docs pages were captured after the run, without server publication timestamps or archive snapshots. That means the record does not fix what those pages said on April 17 or April 28, 2026—or that those entries existed on those dates. The rebuttal’s “technical report” repeats the same pages as they read now; it does not supply independent, time‑fixed proof. Without time‑fixing, the Council cannot reliably place the alleged official communication before the January 1, 2027 00:00 PST bar.

2) Issuer control and “official launch by Polymarket” are not proven. The Contracts page excerpt (PX‑1/PX‑2) describes a “single source of truth for addresses used across the platform” and lists “pUSD — CollateralToken (proxy).” That is an integration/roster function; it does not itself prove Polymarket issued the token or controls its admin keys. The record contains no deployer/admin provenance mapping the token to a Polymarket‑controlled multisig or previously attested release address. The plaintiff leans on an explorer label (“Polymarket: Deployer 1”) referenced in argument; explorer metadata is editable and, without on‑chain key mapping in evidence, cannot carry issuer attribution under preponderance.

3) Fungibility/transferability and exclusions remain unresolved. The concept page language quoted in PX‑1/PX‑2 says “standard ERC‑20” and “Transferable — Yes,” but there is no contract code, ABI, or transaction analysis in the record demonstrating open EOA↔EOA transfers rather than protocol‑permissioned movement. The same pages characterize pUSD as collateral “backed by USDC,” consistent with an internal settlement wrapper. On this record, the possibility that pUSD functions like an internal balance/points‑style wrapper has not been dispelled, and exclusions caution against counting such systems. Unresolved material questions cut against the proponent’s burden.

4) Official communication of a token launch is ambiguous. Even accepting the quoted text at face value, the April 17 entry merely “targets” a go‑live and the April 28 entry states “CLOB V2 is live” and points to a migration guide. That is a system upgrade notice. The record contains no unambiguous, dated Polymarket announcement that it launched “Polymarket USD (pUSD)” as a token on that date. On a proposition that requires public communication of the token’s launch, ambiguity matters—especially given the timing/authentication gap above.

Addressing the plaintiff’s best points
- “Single source of truth” + named pUSD + address: A first‑party address roster shows what the platform integrates; it is not the same as proof of issuance/launch responsibility. Without deployer/admin mapping, control by Polymarket is not more likely than not.
- Dated changelog/migration entries: The plaintiff’s own exhibit acknowledges the absence of server timestamps and archival captures. With no time‑fixed evidence, the Council cannot confidently place those communications before the bar.
- Explorer corroboration: Labels on a block explorer are not authoritative issuer attestation in this record and do not cure the missing on‑chain provenance.

Bottom line
To satisfy the proposition, the plaintiff needed time‑fixed official communications and on‑chain provenance tying the token to Polymarket control, plus reliable proof of open, user‑transferable fungibility outside the exclusions—all before January 1, 2027 00:00 PST. Instead, the record offers mutable docs pages without independent timestamps, an integration roster without issuer provenance, and ambiguous upgrade language. Under a preponderance standard, those gaps mean the elements are not more likely than not satisfied.
