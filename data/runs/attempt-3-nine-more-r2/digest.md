## Filings

### Openings

[plaintiff] Council, this case asks a narrow, verifiable question under a preponderance standard: Is it more likely than not that, before January 1, 2027 at 00:00 PST, Polymarket launched an official fungible token and publicly communicated that launch through its own channels, with NFTs, promotional items, point systems, and partner/community tokens excluded?

Plaintiff’s theory
- Polymarket did launch an official fungible token before the cutoff and said so through its own channels. The decisive proof will pair (1) time‑stamped official communications with (2) on‑chain evidence attributable to Polymarket and (3) indications of public availability consistent with a launch.

What “official fungible token” and “launch” mean
- Fungible token: a transferable cryptoasset on a public blockchain (e.g., ERC‑20/SPL‑like), not an NFT and not non‑transferable points.
- Official: created, issued, or adopted by Polymarket (or its operating entity) and publicly communicated on Polymarket‑controlled channels (website, blog, docs, product UI, verified social, or press releases under its control). Third‑party announcements alone are insufficient.
- Launch: public release—e.g., announcement with a contract address or ticker; enabling transferability, distribution, listing, redemption, or similar availability. Mere internal testing or silent contract deployment is not enough; clear official communication plus observable availability is.

Key factual questions
1) Did Polymarket’s official channels, before the cutoff, communicate that a Polymarket fungible token was launched, naming its ticker or contract?
2) Is there an on‑chain fungible token whose deployer/owner or mint authority is credibly attributable to Polymarket (corporate wallet, multisig, verified contract linked by Polymarket)?
3) Was the token publicly available (listing, distribution, redemption, transfers) before the cutoff?
4) Do materials indicate the asset was instead an NFT, off‑chain/non‑transferable points, a purely promotional voucher, or a partner/community token rather than a Polymarket token?
5) Timing: Do best‑evidence timestamps (official posts, archived pages, block times) place both announcement and availability before the cutoff in PST?

Proof that will satisfy the burden
- Primary: A Polymarket‑controlled announcement before the cutoff explicitly presenting a fungible token as a Polymarket token and identifying its contract or ticker, paired with chain‑explorer data showing the token existed and was usable or distributed before the cutoff.
- Corroboration: Archived snapshots; consistent messaging across official channels; press coverage linking to Polymarket’s announcement; code or repos under Polymarket control referencing the address; attestations tying the deployer to Polymarket.
- Authentication: Evidence tying communicating accounts and deployer wallets to Polymarket (domain control, verified handles, on‑site linking, cryptographic signatures).

Proof that would defeat the claim
- Only points, NFTs, or promotional credits existed before the cutoff; any fungible token appeared only after; or the token was launched by a third party without official Polymarket adoption. Also defeating would be clear official statements before the cutoff that no token had been launched.

Why this resolves the real dispute
- The dispute turns on whether “official” and “launch” are met by Polymarket’s own communications and control over a fungible token, not third‑party hype. The evidence above answers attribution, asset type, and timing against the exclusions.

Next steps to build the record
- Collect and authenticate: (a) Polymarket website/blog/docs/app UI notices; (b) posts from verified official social accounts; (c) archived snapshots prior to the cutoff; (d) chain‑explorer records (contract creation, minting, ownership, distributions) and deployer provenance; (e) official press releases or emails; (f) corroborating reputable press citing Polymarket’s communications. Convert all timestamps to PST and correlate announcement with availability. Obtain cryptographic proofs, if needed, that a deployer or multisig is controlled by Polymarket.

Under a preponderance standard, a coherent set of official communications plus matching on‑chain facts will suffice; the absence of such official communication, or the presence only of excluded categories, will not.

[defendant] Council, the proposition is conjunctive under a preponderance standard: before 00:00 PST on Jan 1, 2027, Polymarket (1) launched (2) an official (3) fungible token and (4) publicly communicated that launch via Polymarket‑controlled channels. NFTs, promos, point systems, and partner/community tokens are excluded. If any element is unproven, the proposition fails.

Defense theory
The more likely scenario is that no such Polymarket‑official fungible token launch—paired with explicit, pre‑cutoff communication by Polymarket—occurred. Items likely to be cited (off‑chain points, per‑market instruments, third‑party or partner tokens, or rumor‑driven posts) either fall within exclusions or fail attribution, launch, or timing.

Disputed elements
- “Official”: whether authorship, control, or adoption is attributable to Polymarket (or its operating entity) rather than unaffiliated deployers or partners.
- “Launch”: whether there was a public release with availability (distribution, transferability, listing) rather than a silent/test deployment.
- “Publicly communicated through official channels”: whether Polymarket’s own site, blog, docs, product UI, or verified social accounts conveyed the launch (third‑party reporting is insufficient).
- Timing: whether both communication and availability pre‑date the cutoff once timestamps are normalized to PST.

Factual questions that will decide the case
1) Did Polymarket itself state, on a Polymarket‑controlled channel before the cutoff, that a Polymarket fungible token had launched, identifying its ticker/contract or role?
2) Is there on‑chain evidence of a fungible token whose deployer/owner or mint authority is credibly attributable to Polymarket (corporate wallet, multisig, domain‑verified contract) before the cutoff?
3) Was the token publicly available pre‑cutoff (transfers enabled, distribution, redemption, or listing), not just inert or test code?
4) Do proffered items fall within exclusions—NFTs, promos, non‑transferable/off‑chain point systems—or are they tokens launched by partners/communities rather than Polymarket?
5) Are there pre‑cutoff official statements by Polymarket indicating no token launch or clarifying that points/promos are not fungible tokens?

Proof that would satisfy plaintiff’s burden
- Primary: A pre‑cutoff Polymarket‑controlled announcement explicitly presenting a Polymarket fungible token as launched, plus chain‑explorer data confirming existence, Polymarket control, and public availability pre‑cutoff. 
- Corroboration: Archived snapshots, consistent cross‑channel messaging, and press that links back to Polymarket’s announcement. 
- Authentication: Ties between deployer/mint authority and Polymarket (domain‑verified addresses, cross‑links from official properties, or cryptographic attestations).

Proof that defeats the proposition
- No pre‑cutoff Polymarket communication announcing a fungible token launch; or communications referencing only excluded categories (NFTs, promos, points). 
- On‑chain tokens bearing “Polymarket” but deployed by unaffiliated third parties/partners without Polymarket’s official adoption and announcement. 
- Pre‑cutoff deployments without Polymarket communication, or with no public availability until after the cutoff. 
- Official Polymarket statements pre‑cutoff that no token had launched or that any referenced asset is not a fungible token.

Record‑building steps
- Collect Polymarket‑controlled materials (website/blog/docs, app UI notices, verified social, official press); preserve timestamps and archives; convert to PST. 
- Obtain chain‑explorer records (creation time, ownership, minting, transfers, verifications); map controlling wallets; look for links from official Polymarket properties. 
- Build a timeline aligning communications with on‑chain facts; note gaps between announcement and availability. 
- Identify and exclude points, NFTs, promos, or partner/community tokens, documenting why each exclusion applies. 
- Search for pre‑cutoff Polymarket statements clarifying absence of a token or non‑fungibility of points.

Why plaintiff is unlikely to prevail
Because the claim requires official authorship/adoption, an actual launch, and explicit pre‑cutoff communication by Polymarket, ambiguity on attribution, asset category, or timing is likely. Under a preponderance standard, unresolved ambiguity on any element defeats the proposition.

Issue and burden
The proposition is conjunctive. Plaintiff must show, by a preponderance of the evidence, that before January 1, 2027 at 00:00 PST, Polymarket (1) launched (2) an official (3) fungible token and (4) publicly communicated that launch through Polymarket‑controlled channels, with the listed exclusions.

Defense position
On this record, plaintiff has not crossed 50% on two elements: “launched” and “officially released by Polymarket.” The materials credibly show Polymarket documented a new collateral instrument called “Polymarket USD (pUSD)” and rolled it out with CLOB V2. They do not reliably establish that Polymarket launched a Polymarket token in the sense required by the proposition—i.e., that Polymarket itself issued/released a fungible cryptoasset as a token launch, as opposed to introducing an internal collateral mechanism within a backend upgrade.

What the record shows (and what it does not)
1) Polymarket docs describe pUSD as the collateral token used for all trading, a standard ERC‑20 on Polygon, transferable, backed by USDC. (primary‑evidence.txt; official‑source‑record.txt)
2) A Polymarket “Contracts” page, styled as a “single source of truth,” lists a pUSD contract address. (official‑source‑record.txt)
3) Changelog and a V2 migration page say CLOB V2 went live April 28, 2026 and reference “a new collateral token (pUSD).” (primary‑evidence.txt; official‑source‑record.txt)
4) A Polygonscan page is cited as corroboration. (primary‑evidence.txt; official‑source‑record.txt)

What is missing for plaintiff’s burden
A) Launch vs. infrastructure change: Nothing in the cited Polymarket pages frames this as a “token launch.” The communications characterize pUSD as a collateral component of an infrastructure release, not a stand‑alone token product launch. There is no Polymarket announcement that “we launched a Polymarket token,” no distribution/issuance details, and no evidence of a public release separate from enabling the exchange backend. (primary‑evidence.txt)
B) “Officially released by Polymarket”: The record lacks chain‑level attribution that Polymarket deployed or controls the pUSD token contract (deployer wallet, admin/mint authority, or cryptographic attestation). A link from an official docs page to an address is adoption/usage evidence; it is not proof that Polymarket “released” the token as the issuer. (official‑source‑record.txt)
C) Timing authentication: Plaintiff relies on Polymarket‑authored dates in a changelog and migration page. The preserved copies are post‑run captures without immutable archive timestamps or server headers tying those statements to April 2026. No block‑timestamp evidence for contract creation or first public availability is in the record. (official‑source‑record.txt; unresolved‑record‑gaps.txt)

Why these gaps matter to the elements
- “Launched”: The proposition requires a launch of a token, not merely the introduction of an internal collateral unit as part of a code upgrade. A reasonable reading of “launch” entails a public release of a token as a product/asset—identified issuance, availability, and circulation—not just a backend refactor. The record never uses “launch a token” language and supplies no issuance/distribution facts. At best, it shows that Polymarket enabled trading collateral labeled pUSD when V2 went live. That is consistent with an infrastructure change, not a token launch.
- “Officially released by Polymarket”: Even if pUSD exists and is used, the record does not demonstrate Polymarket authored/deployed the ERC‑20, held the mint/admin keys, or otherwise “released” it as its issuer. Without that, plaintiff has not proved this was a Polymarket token release rather than a Polymarket‑documented integration or wrapper.
- “Publicly communicated through official channels”: The docs are indeed official channels, but they communicate an exchange upgrade that includes a collateral token—again pointing to infrastructure, not an official token launch.

Addressing plaintiff’s points
- Fungibility: We do not contest that an ERC‑20 is fungible. The dispute is “official” and “launch.”
- Single‑source‑of‑truth page: This is strong evidence of Polymarket adopting/using the instrument; it is not sufficient to prove Polymarket released/issued a token.
- Explorer corroboration: The record describes but does not preserve immutable on‑chain creation timestamps, deployer attribution, or governance of the contract. Without those, chain corroboration does not cure the authorship/launch gap.
- Timing: Even crediting April 28, 2026 as V2 go‑live, plaintiff still must prove a token “launch” by Polymarket. The record does not do so.

Inference and standard
Where language is ambiguous, the burden stays with plaintiff. On this packet, a fair tie‑breaker is to require clear evidence that Polymarket actually launched a Polymarket token (issuance/release) and said so. Because the current proof is equally consistent with an internal collateral mechanism shipped in a backend upgrade, the probability that the proposition is true does not exceed 50%.

Conclusion
Plaintiff proved that Polymarket documented and adopted a collateral ERC‑20 called pUSD in connection with CLOB V2. Plaintiff did not prove, by a preponderance, that Polymarket launched an official fungible token within the meaning of the proposition—i.e., that Polymarket itself released/issued a token and publicly communicated that launch. The Council should find the proposition not proven on this record.

2) “Officially released by Polymarket” is unproven: The proposition’s own language requires official release by Polymarket. The record shows adoption and documentation, not authorship or issuance. There is no chain‑level attribution tying deployer, owner, mint/admin authority, or governance of the ERC‑20 to Polymarket. A contracts registry showing what the platform uses proves use; it does not prove that Polymarket released/issued the token. DX‑1 flags these attribution gaps.

3) Communication about what: We do not dispute there was official communication—about the V2 upgrade and the existence/role of pUSD as collateral. What is missing is communication that Polymarket launched and officially released a token, as the proposition requires.

4) Timing does not cure authorship: Even if April 28, 2026 is accepted (and the UTC→Pacific conversion is uncontested), timing only matters if a qualifying launch by Polymarket occurred. The authorship/launch gap remains.

Addressing plaintiff’s strongest point
Plaintiff argues that “shipped a new collateral token” plus production go‑live equals a token launch. That argument reads “officially released by Polymarket” out of the proposition. “Release” connotes issuer‑level action or origination attributable to Polymarket, not merely documenting and enabling a collateral instrument within a backend upgrade. Without issuer attribution or a statement that Polymarket released a token, the evidence is in equipoise between a collateral component activation and a token launch. Under a preponderance standard, equipoise is insufficient.

Exclusions
Whether pUSD is not an NFT, not points, etc., does not resolve the missing elements of launch and official release.

Conclusion
The record demonstrates a Polymarket‑documented, branded ERC‑20 used as collateral and activated with CLOB V2. It does not demonstrate that Polymarket “launched” and “officially released” a token in the sense the proposition requires. Because the key facts are at least equally consistent with a collateral component rollout as with a token launch by Polymarket, plaintiff has not crossed 50%. The Council should find the proposition not proven.
