## Filings

### Openings

[plaintiff] Council,

Issue and theory
Did Polymarket, before January 1, 2027 at 00:00 PST, launch an official fungible token and publicly communicate that launch via its own channels? Plaintiff’s theory: on the balance of probabilities, yes—if the record can pair an on‑chain fungible token with Polymarket’s own dated communications.

Working definitions (aligned to the proposition)
- Official fungible token: a transferable, interchangeable cryptoasset (e.g., ERC‑20/SPL/analog) that Polymarket created or authorized as its own. “Official” requires issuer/control attribution to Polymarket and self‑identification as such.
- Publicly communicated through official channels: statements traceable to Polymarket‑controlled outlets (website/blog/help center/app UI/verified social/email/legal or technical docs). Third‑party posts alone are insufficient.
- Launched: (1) token contract exists on a production chain, (2) Polymarket holds it out as its token via official channels, and (3) the token is made publicly available beyond closed tests. All three are required because the proposition demands an official release and public communication.
- Exclusions (per proposition): NFTs; purely promotional trinkets; tokenized point systems that are not on‑chain fungible tokens; and tokens primarily launched by another project/community where Polymarket is merely a collaborator.

Disputed questions that will decide the case
1) Existence/identity: Is there a specific fungible token (name/symbol/contract/chain) uniquely tied to Polymarket as issuer/controller?
2) Official communication: Did Polymarket, via its channels, represent that token as its own?
3) Timing: Do reliable timestamps show both the on‑chain launch and Polymarket’s public communication occurred before 00:00 PST on January 1, 2027? (With explicit PST conversion.)
4) Control/authorization: Do governance/ownership materials show Polymarket control, as opposed to a partner’s asset?
5) Exclusions: Is the asset an NFT, off‑chain points, a promo, or primarily another party’s token? If so, the proposition fails.

Proof that would satisfy the preponderance standard
- Dated Polymarket communications identifying the token as Polymarket’s (ideally with contract/chain details), plus on‑chain records evidencing contract creation, initial mint/distribution, and public availability before the cutoff.
- Corroborators linking control to Polymarket: verified contract publisher/owner, multisig attributable to Polymarket, official docs or repos referencing the address, in‑app notices, help‑center articles, or legal pages.
- Consistent independent coverage that quotes/links to Polymarket’s materials may support dating and context, but cannot replace an official communication.

Proof that would defeat the proposition
- No Polymarket communication identifying a fungible token before the cutoff, or official disclaimers during the period.
- Evidence that the referenced asset is an NFT, a non‑transferable/off‑chain points program, or a limited promotional coupon.
- Evidence the on‑chain deployment, public announcement, or public availability occurred on/after the cutoff.
- Evidence the token is primarily another project’s asset, with Polymarket not presenting it as its own.

Forward‑looking evidence plan
- Date‑stamped captures from Polymarket‑controlled outlets referencing an official token and, ideally, a contract address.
- Chain‑explorer records (contract creation, supply events, ownership) with block‑time to PST conversions.
- Materials evidencing issuer/control (contract owner keys, governance, or organizational attributions).
- Any official denials/clarifications to test exclusions.

Roadmap and requested finding
Plaintiff will organize the record under the three‑prong “launch” test (on‑chain existence, official communication, public availability) and the exclusions screen. If the record establishes a specific token and a Polymarket announcement—both pre‑cutoff—the Council should find the proposition true. If that linkage or timing is missing, the proposition is not proven.

[defendant] Council,

Position and theory
The proposition is narrow: before 2027‑01‑01 at 00:00 PST, Polymarket launched an official fungible token and publicly communicated that launch via its own channels. Defendant’s theory is that the claim fails unless the record ties a specific on‑chain fungible token to Polymarket’s own, date‑verifiable statements, and shows public availability before the cutoff, while clearing the stated exclusions (NFTs, promos, off‑chain points, and partner/community issuances). On preponderance, any material gap on identity, attribution, timing, or exclusions defeats the claim.

What the dispute is really about
1) Meaning of “official fungible token” and “launched”: whether “official” requires Polymarket’s issuer/control attribution and self‑identification, and “launched” requires production deployment, Polymarket’s self‑identification, and public availability beyond closed tests.
2) Attribution/control: whether the contract (address/chain/standard) can be linked to Polymarket’s control or explicit authorization rather than a partner, community, or impersonator.
3) Official channels: whether the communication appears on Polymarket‑controlled outlets (site/blog/help center/docs/app UI/legal pages or verified corporate social). Third‑party media, exchange pages, aggregators, and employee personal accounts are insufficient without a Polymarket‑controlled statement.
4) Timing and time zone: whether both the on‑chain launch and the Polymarket communication are proven to have occurred strictly before 2027‑01‑01 00:00 PST, with reliable PST conversion and guardrails against indexing/capture artifacts.
5) Exclusions: whether an identified asset is, instead, an NFT, a limited promo, an off‑chain points/credits system, or primarily another party’s token.

Decisive factual questions
- Identity: exact name/symbol, chain, and contract address; proof the asset is a fungible on‑chain token (e.g., ERC‑20/SPL/analog), not market shares, IOUs, or testnet.
- Polymarket self‑identification: the specific page/post/UI/legal doc where Polymarket represents the token as its own, with immutable or archived timestamps.
- Control: deployer/owner/admin keys or multisig traceable to Polymarket; official docs or repos referencing the contract.
- Launch/public availability: evidence of distribution/use accessible to the public (not merely internal/whitelisted) before the cutoff.
- Timing proof: block‑time records and PST conversions for deployment/mint/transfers; PST‑stamped timing of Polymarket communications.
- Exclusions screen: whether the asset falls into any excluded category or originates primarily from a collaborator/community.

Proof that would satisfy the burden
- A production‑chain contract address with explorer records (creation/mint/first transfers) pre‑cutoff, plus a Polymarket‑controlled, date‑verifiable communication explicitly identifying the token as Polymarket’s, ideally referencing the contract.
- Evidence tying control to Polymarket (verified publisher/owner or multisig, official docs/repos, or in‑app/legal references).
- Records of public availability pre‑cutoff, with clear timestamps and non‑test access.

Proof that would defeat the proposition
- No Polymarket‑controlled communication identifying any fungible token as its own before the cutoff, or official disclaimers during the period.
- Evidence the cited asset is an NFT, a promo voucher, an off‑chain/non‑transferable points/credits/referral system, or a partner/community token not held out as Polymarket’s.
- On‑chain or communication timestamps on/after 2027‑01‑01 00:00 PST, or failure to tie the asset to a unique contract under Polymarket control.
- Impersonations/ticker collisions, exchange IOUs/wrappers, or testnets posed as mainnet.

Forward‑looking plan
- Collect date‑stamped captures from Polymarket‑controlled outlets explicitly naming any token and, ideally, its contract.
- Obtain chain‑explorer records; convert block times to PST; document initial mint/distribution and ownership keys.
- Gather organizational materials linking control to Polymarket and any official denials/clarifications.
- Apply the exclusions screen to any candidate asset.

Decision rule
Plaintiff must supply a coherent, time‑consistent chain from on‑chain identity and control to Polymarket’s own dated communication and public availability, all before the cutoff and not excluded. Absent that, the proposition is not proven.

Claim, timing, and burden
The proposition is historical: before January 1, 2027 at 00:00 PST, Polymarket launched an official fungible token and publicly communicated that launch through Polymarket’s own channels. Plaintiff bears the burden to prove, on the preponderance of the evidence, all required elements: (1) a specific on‑chain fungible token attributable to Polymarket (identity, chain, contract, and control), (2) Polymarket’s own public communication identifying it as Polymarket’s token, and (3) public availability before the cutoff. What the record actually contains (and does not)
• There are no captured official Polymarket pages, posts, contract listings, explorer pages, or archives in evidence. Element‑by‑element defects
1) Identity and control. The record lacks a captured contract address on a Polymarket‑controlled “single source of truth,” any verified‑publisher/owner data, or even a minimal explorer record. A bare hexadecimal address in a brief is not probative; explorer labels are frequently user‑edited or stale, and contract proxies add further ambiguity. Without authenticated source captures, attribution to Polymarket is unproven.
2) Official communication. The proposition requires that Polymarket publicly communicated the token launch via its own channels. No captured page/post/UI/legal notice from a Polymarket‑controlled outlet is in evidence. No server‑dated page, no verified social, no app banner, and no archive timestamps appear in the packet. 3) Launch timing before the PST cutoff. The record provides no block numbers, block times, mint/transfer history, or PST conversions. Plaintiff references “~11:00 UTC on April 28, 2026” via an uncaptured changelog. That is not in evidence, and it also raises the PST/PDT issue the proposition itself creates: April 28, 2026 falls in Pacific Daylight Time, not Pacific Standard Time, so precise conversions and dated artifacts matter. 4) Public availability. There is no evidence the alleged token was accessible to the public (as opposed to an internal or whitelisted artifact) before the cutoff—no explorer transfers, no contract read/write restrictions, no distribution records.
5) Exclusions screen. The packet has no proof that the asset (if it exists) is an on‑chain fungible token rather than an off‑chain points/credit balance, a promo instrument, or something primarily launched by another project. Assertions in briefing do not carry this burden without source records.

About adverse material cited by plaintiff
Plaintiff references a Polymarket Help Center line (“does not have a token”) and then attempts to cabin it by date. That text is also not in evidence. More importantly, neither the purported disclaimer nor counsel’s interpretation of later events can substitute for captured, dated, Polymarket‑controlled communications and on‑chain records establishing a launch before the cutoff.

Under our agreed launch test (identity+control, official communication, public availability+timing), plaintiff has tendered zero authenticated exhibits or technical reports. On this packet, the Council cannot credit uncaptured web pages, explorer labels, or unsourced contract numbers. On this record, the Council should find the proposition not established by a preponderance of the evidence and rule against it. Requested disposition: Find that the proposition is not established on the present record. If the proponent later supplies the actual captured pages and on‑chain timestamping tying identity, official communication, and timing together, the Council can reassess; today, the evidence is insufficient.
