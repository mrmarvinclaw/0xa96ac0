### Openings

[plaintiff] To the Council:

Issue and burden. The proposition asks whether, before January 1, 2027 at 00:00 PST, Polymarket launched an “official fungible token,” meaning: (a) a fungible token (not an NFT), (b) officially released by Polymarket, and (c) publicly communicated through Polymarket’s official channels, while excluding NFTs, promotional offerings, tokenized points, and launches in collaboration with other projects/communities. Standard: preponderance of the evidence.

Plaintiff’s theory. A qualifying “launch” requires two conjunctive elements completed before the cutoff: (1) official release by Polymarket (organizational action/control) and (2) public communication of that release through Polymarket’s official channels. Mere on‑chain existence, third‑party mints, speculative media, or employee personal posts are insufficient.

Core dispute. The case will be decided by whether any token satisfies all of: officiality, official communication, timing, fungibility, and non‑exclusion. The question is not whether a token exists somewhere on‑chain, but whether Polymarket itself launched and publicly presented it as official before the cutoff.

Decisive factual questions:
1) Token existence and type: Is there a specific fungible token contract (with standard and metadata) attributable to Polymarket?
2) Attribution/control: Do credible materials tie the deployer/owner/treasury or issuer authority to Polymarket as an organization?
3) Official communication: Did Polymarket itself, via official channels (website/app, documentation, blog/newsroom, help center, verified brand social accounts, in‑app notices, or release notes), publicly present the token as an official Polymarket release?
4) Timing: Did both issuance and the qualifying official communication occur before 00:00 PST on January 1, 2027? Are timestamps reliably normalized to PST and corroborated?
5) Exclusion screening: Does the asset avoid exclusion as an NFT, a promotional incentive, a tokenized points/credits system, or a collaboration‑led token where Polymarket did not itself “officially release” it?

What proof will satisfy the burden:
- On‑chain records: token contract address; standard; creation/mint/owner transactions; verified source/metadata; deployer/owner roles; block timestamps.
- First‑party communications: dated materials on Polymarket’s official channels (site/app, docs, blog, newsroom, help center, product release notes, verified brand social) that explicitly characterize the token as an official Polymarket release.
- Attribution linkage: documentation or statements mapping official Polymarket addresses to the token, or consistent reuse of known treasury/deployer addresses in prior official releases.
- Archival corroboration: time‑stamped snapshots of the above materials (e.g., web archives) fixing both content and date; product UI captures indicating in‑app announcements.
- Integration evidence: build/release artifacts, terms/FAQ changes, or API/docs updates that incorporate the token as part of Polymarket’s product.

What proof would defeat the claim:
- Evidence that any token activity was purely third‑party/community‑issued or primarily a partner’s launch where Polymarket lacked release authority.
- Evidence the asset was points, credits, market shares, testnet/play tokens, NFTs, or promotions without an official token launch.
- Evidence that either the issuance or the necessary official communication occurred only after the cutoff, or that no official communication exists.
- Evidence of misattributed/inauthentic channels or back‑dated/edited materials lacking trustworthy archival proof.

Evaluation principles for the Council:
- Give greatest weight to primary on‑chain records and first‑party communications. Treat third‑party coverage as corroboration only when it clearly relies on primary sources.
- Reconcile time across sources; use on‑chain time for issuance and archived first‑party timestamps for communications; normalize all to PST.
- Consider “launch” satisfied only if both official release and official communication by Polymarket are present before the cutoff. If either element is missing, the proposition fails.

Requested evidence to efficiently resolve the case:
- A dated, archived official Polymarket communication that describes the token as an official Polymarket release.
- Definitive on‑chain records for the token (contract, standard, creation/mint, owner/deployer) and a mapping tying those to Polymarket.
- Official documentation or product materials clarifying purpose and status to rule out exclusions.
- Any contrary official statements by Polymarket about having or not having a token, to test whether later communications superseded them before the cutoff.

If the Council obtains those items and they align, the preponderance standard will be met for the plaintiff; if they show only third‑party tokens, points, promotions, collaborations, or post‑cutoff communications, the proposition should be rejected.

Bottom line. Because the proposition is conjunctive and time‑bound, any gap in organizational authority, official communication, or reliable timing leaves the evidence below preponderance. The Council should require precise, dated, first‑party proof tying a specific fungible token to Polymarket’s release and announcement before the cutoff; otherwise, the proposition should be rejected.
