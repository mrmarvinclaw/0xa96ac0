### Openings

[plaintiff] Council, the question is narrow and dispositive: Before January 1, 2027 at 00:00 PST, did Polymarket launch an official fungible token and publicly communicate that launch through its own official channels? Our theory is yes. Under a preponderance standard, we aim to show it is more likely than not that Polymarket released and announced a transferable, platform‑wide fungible token that it itself authorized.

The real dispute
- What counts as “official fungible token”: a transferable, fungible cryptoasset (e.g., ERC‑20 or equivalent) serving as a platform‑level token, not a per‑market outcome share, not an NFT, not a coupon or promotional instrument, and not a wrapped points balance.
- What counts as “officially released and publicly communicated”: Polymarket (or an entity it controls) launched or authorized the token and disclosed that fact via its official website, blog, documentation, press page, verified social accounts, or other official channels it controls.
- What is excluded: (a) NFTs; (b) promotional offerings; (c) tokenized point systems; (d) token launches principally “in collaboration with” another project or community such that the token is not Polymarket’s own platform token.

Decisive factual questions
1) Existence and identity: Is there a specific fungible token claimed as Polymarket’s platform token, with a stable name/ticker and on‑chain address?
2) Issuer authorization/control: Do reliable indicators link the deployer, mint, or admin authority to Polymarket or its controlled entities, rather than an unrelated third party?
3) Official communication before the cutoff: Did an official Polymarket channel, with verifiable timestamps, identify the token before 00:00 PST on January 1, 2027? Time zone conversion must be explicit to enforce the cutoff.
4) Nature of the asset: Does the token function platform‑wide, as opposed to outcome shares, NFTs, coupons, or points derivatives? Is it independent of a co‑branded launch that would trigger the exclusion?
5) Distribution and framing: Was the initial distribution characterized as a token launch rather than a mere promotion or points wrapper?

Proof that will satisfy the burden
- Official‑channel communications: Items on Polymarket’s primary domain(s), blog, docs/FAQ, press room, verified social accounts, or official community announcements under Polymarket’s control, each with preserved timestamps predating the deadline; ideally identifying the token by name/ticker and linking to its contract or a canonical page.
- On‑chain corroboration: A verified token contract with creation/mint times before the deadline and provenance linking to Polymarket‑controlled addresses; or cryptographic statements from Polymarket that bind its identity to the contract address.
- Archival authenticity: Web archives or platform‑native timestamps to fix timing; consistent cross‑references between on‑chain data and official announcements.

What would defeat our claim
- No official Polymarket communication identifying a platform token before the deadline.
- Evidence that the only relevant assets were: per‑market outcome tokens; NFTs; points or non‑transferable balances; or a token principally launched by/with another project such that it is not Polymarket’s own platform token.
- A clear, dated official disavowal during the relevant period that no such token existed.

Anticipated defenses and responses
- “Outcome shares are fungible.” They are fungible within a market but are not the platform’s official token; they lack platform‑wide identity and purpose. We will show the distinction the proposition requires.
- “A partner/community issued a ‘Polymarket’ coin.” Excluded absent Polymarket’s own authorization and official channel communications.
- “It was only points.” The proposition excludes tokenized point systems; we will analyze branding, purpose, and technical properties to distinguish a platform token from a points wrapper.

Forward‑looking evidence plan
- Enumerate Polymarket’s official domains and verified social handles; collect dated announcements that name the token and, ideally, the contract.
- Retrieve on‑chain records: contract address, creation block/time, deployer, initial holders, admin roles, and links to Polymarket‑controlled wallets.
- Normalize all timestamps to PST to evaluate the 2027‑01‑01 00:00 cutoff.
- Seek and preserve contrary official statements to fairly test the exclusions and timing.

Under the preponderance standard, this case will turn on whether official communications and on‑chain provenance, taken together, make it more likely than not that Polymarket launched and publicly announced its own fungible platform token before the stated cutoff. That is the showing we intend to make.

   The Contracts page being a “single source of truth” for addresses evidences code lineage, not that Polymarket “released” pUSD as its official platform token.    Even accepting that the changelog/migration pages were public on April 28, 2026, what they communicate is a system upgrade that includes a collateral token—not that Polymarket launched a token. Under a fair reading of the proposition, “launching an official fungible token” requires more than deploying a collateral wrapper contract and mentioning it in technical docs. The requirement is “publicly communicated that launch,” i.e., a communication about the launch of the token itself. The record stops short of that.

4) The exclusions reinforce the intended boundary. The proposition excludes NFTs, promotions, tokenized point systems, and partner/community coins—signals that the framers were cabining “official token” to a native Polymarket asset released as such. A USDC‑backed collateral receipt used for settlement is a different category; counting it would render the exclusions toothless and make the proposition trivially satisfied by back‑end plumbing.

5) Timing proofs do not cure the content gap. DX‑1 notes the absence of immutable timestamps and server headers. But the decisive defect is not timing; it is what the pages say. Even crediting the dates and the go‑live conversion, the communications still describe an exchange upgrade that employs a collateral token—not a token launch.

Addressing the plaintiff’s strongest point
The best plaintiff evidence is the April 2026 language that Polymarket was “shipping … a new collateral token (pUSD)” and that “CLOB V2 is live on production,” plus the Contracts entry. But “shipping collateral as part of CLOB V2” is not the same as “launching an official Polymarket token,” and a contracts index is not a launch announcement. Under a preponderance standard, where two reasonable readings remain—(a) internal collateral wrapper shipped with an upgrade (the record’s plain framing) and (b) an official token launch—the tie goes to not proven.

Conclusion
On this record, it is proven that Polymarket’s CLOB V2 went live on April 28, 2026 and that it employs pUSD, an ERC‑20 collateral token listed on Polymarket’s contracts page. It is not proven that Polymarket launched—and publicly communicated the launch of—an official Polymarket fungible token within the meaning of the proposition. The evidence is at best equivocal on the critical “launch and public communication of the launch” element. Under preponderance, that is insufficient. The proposition should be found NOT PROVEN.
