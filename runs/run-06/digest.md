## Filings

### Openings

[plaintiff] Council members,

This case asks a narrow, verifiable question: before 00:00 PST on January 1, 2027, did Polymarket itself launch an official fungible token and publicly communicate that launch via Polymarket-controlled channels, excluding NFTs, promos, point systems, and tokens whose primary issuer is a partner or community? Our theory is yes, and under a preponderance standard we need only show it is more likely than not.

What is actually disputed
- Whether “official,” “fungible token,” and “launched” are met by Polymarket’s own actions and communications, not third‑party chatter.
- Whether any candidate asset is excluded (NFT, promo, points, or primarily another project’s token) even if Polymarket mentioned it.
- Whether reliable timestamps place both on‑chain availability and official communication before the cutoff when normalized to PST.

Guiding definitions for the record
- Official: Communication on channels Polymarket controls (primary website, blog/docs, verified social accounts, official community announcements, in‑app notices) that would lead a reasonable user to attribute issuance to Polymarket.
- Fungible token: A transferable cryptoasset implementing a standard like ERC‑20 (or analogous L1/L2 standard), not an NFT or non‑transferable points, and not merely a generic settlement asset outside Polymarket’s issuance.
- Launched: Publicly released and identified by Polymarket as its token. Announcement without a deployed token, or a silent contract without official identification, does not suffice.
- Exclusions: NFTs, promotions/coupons, tokenized points (even if later made transferable), and tokens primarily issued by partners/communities with Polymarket only integrating or co‑marketing them.

Factual questions that will decide the case
1) Is there a Polymarket‑controlled, time‑stamped communication before the cutoff that clearly identifies a fungible token as Polymarket’s own (name/symbol and ideally contract/chain)?
2) Is there on‑chain evidence of a fungible token contract made publicly available before the cutoff, with credible linkage to Polymarket (e.g., known deployer, verified metadata, or contemporaneous official identification)?
3) Was the token broadly transferable in standard wallets, independent of the app?
4) Do the communications originate from Polymarket channels rather than press, influencers, or partners?
5) Do timestamps (posts and on‑chain) converted to PST predate the deadline?
6) Do any exclusions apply notwithstanding Polymarket mentions?

Proof that would satisfy the burden
- Chain‑explorer records of a fungible token contract and initial distribution events before the cutoff, paired with Polymarket’s official communication uniquely tying to that asset (by name/symbol/contract/chain). Evidence that the deployer or controlling multisig is Polymarket‑associated strengthens attribution.
- Archived Polymarket web pages, blog/docs, or in‑app notices with contemporaneous timestamps identifying the token as official.
- Official social posts or verified community announcements that either link the contract or identify the token in a way that maps uniquely to the on‑chain asset.

Proof that would defeat the burden
- No official Polymarket identification of a fungible token before the cutoff.
- The referenced asset is an NFT, non‑transferable/custodial points, or otherwise not a standard fungible token.
- The primary issuer is a partner/community, with Polymarket only integrating or promoting it.
- Timestamps, once normalized to PST, fall after the deadline.

Anticipated disputes and resolution
- “Official channel” ambiguity: favor Polymarket‑controlled properties and verified accounts; require corroboration if ambiguous.
- “Launch” vs “announcement”: require both on‑chain availability and official identification; either alone is insufficient.
- Points‑to‑token conversions or rebrands: post‑cutoff conversions do not retroactively meet the proposition; a pre‑cutoff fungible token with proper identification does.
- Collaborations: co‑marketing is not enough unless Polymarket’s own materials state it is Polymarket’s token and the on‑chain facts align.

Forward‑looking plan
- Collect and archive Polymarket’s official announcements, web pages, docs, and in‑app notices with clear timestamps.
- Identify candidate token(s); gather explorer records (deployment, minting, ownership) and convert timestamps to PST.
- Link official communications to the unique on‑chain asset via name/symbol/contract/chain and, where possible, org‑controlled deployers or verified metadata.
- Surface and address contrary official statements (e.g., disclaimers of any token) by date and scope, to reconcile or distinguish them.

Under the preponderance standard, our task is to assemble the better‑aligned set of Polymarket‑controlled communications and on‑chain facts showing that, more likely than not, Polymarket launched and publicly identified a fungible token before the deadline, while the exclusions do not apply.

[defendant] Council members,

NFTs, promos, point systems, and tokens primarily issued by partners/communities are expressly out.

More likely than not, no evidence will establish both on‑chain issuance by Polymarket and contemporaneous, explicit identification by Polymarket’s own channels within the period. Integrations, speculation, and generic settlement assets do not meet the proposition. Ambiguity should be resolved against the moving party.

Where the real dispute lies

- “Launched” requires a live, transferable fungible token contract publicly available within the window; announcement‑only or silent contracts are insufficient.
- “Fungible token” means a transferable standard (e.g., ERC‑20 analogs) usable in standard wallets; custodial balances and non‑transferable points are out.
- “Official channels” are Polymarket‑controlled properties (site, docs/blog, verified social, in‑app notices). Press, partners, or unverified community posts don’t qualify.
- Time normalization: both the on‑chain fact and the official communication must predate the cutoff when converted to PST.

Factual questions that will decide the case
1) Did a Polymarket‑controlled channel, within the window, explicitly identify a particular fungible token as Polymarket’s own (name/symbol and ideally contract/chain)?
2) Is there an on‑chain fungible token contract live before the cutoff with credible issuer attribution to Polymarket (known deployer/owner, verified metadata)?
3) Is the token broadly transferable in standard wallets, independent of the app?
4) Are any candidates excluded (NFTs, promos, point/credit systems, wrapped/IOU instruments, or partner/community‑issued tokens)?

6) Are there Polymarket communications within the period disclaiming any token; if so, how are they reconciled?
7) Can the identified token be uniquely mapped from the communication to one on‑chain contract without name collisions or cross‑chain ambiguity?

Proof that would satisfy the burden
- A Polymarket‑controlled, time‑stamped communication within the period that clearly states the token is Polymarket’s and uniquely links to the on‑chain asset (by contract/chain or unambiguous identifiers).
- Explorer evidence that the token contract was live pre‑deadline, is transferable, and is credibly attributable to Polymarket (deployer/owner, multisig, verified metadata).

- Absence of explicit, in‑window identification by Polymarket of any fungible token as its own.
- Evidence the asset is an NFT, promo, non‑transferable points/custodial balance, wrapped/mirrored IOU, or primarily issued by a partner/community.
- Ambiguous communications; inability to uniquely link a statement to a single contract; timestamps that fall after the cutoff when normalized; or contrary Polymarket disclaimers within the period.

Anticipated disputes and resolution
- “Official” ambiguity: require explicit ownership/issuance language and Polymarket‑controlled publication.
- “Announcement vs. launch”: require both a live contract and official identification inside the window.
- Name collisions/cross‑chain: demand a unique mapping and credible issuer linkage.
- Points‑to‑token narratives: later conversions/rebrands cannot satisfy a pre‑cutoff launch.

Forward‑looking plan
- Systematically collect Polymarket‑controlled web pages, blogs/docs, verified socials, and in‑app notices; archive with immutable timestamps; normalize all times to PST.
- Identify candidate token(s) across relevant chains; obtain explorer records (deployment, ownership, minting) and confirm transferability in standard wallets.
- Establish organizational attribution (known deployers, multisig signers, verified metadata, or cryptographic links) tying any contract to Polymarket.
- Apply a strict exclusions checklist to each candidate; surface any contrary official statements and place them on a dated timeline.

Given the burden, uncertainty or ambiguity should tip the scales against the proposition unless the plaintiff produces clear, time‑aligned, uniquely linked official communications plus on‑chain issuance by Polymarket itself.

Issue and burden
The proposition requires proof that, before January 1, 2027 at 00:00 PST, Polymarket (i) launched a fungible token, (ii) officially released it, and (iii) publicly communicated that launch via Polymarket‑controlled channels. Why this does not meet the proposition under preponderance
1) “Launched an official fungible token” vs. internal collateral plumbing. The only Polymarket phrasing the record attributes to official channels is “collateral token (pUSD).” That describes an internal settlement instrument used to operate the exchange, not a public token launch as an independent product. The proposition’s ordinary reading contemplates that Polymarket launched a token as such; the record shows an infrastructure upgrade that happens to implement an ERC‑20 wrapper for USDC. Absent explicit self‑identification as “our official token” or a comparable statement of issuance/launch, the plaintiff’s theory relies on inference, not text.
2) Issuer attribution is unproven. The Contracts page summary is an editable documentation page. The record supplies no immutable archive, no deployer/owner address tying the contract to a Polymarket‑controlled multisig, and no cryptographic link. 3) Timing is not immutably established on official channels. The dates cited (April 17 and April 28, 2026) appear only as text in a changelog summarized by PX‑1/PX‑2. There are no server headers, archive snapshots, or signed social posts in evidence. Under preponderance, contemporaneous, immutable timestamps matter; the plaintiff chose not to provide them.
4) The structure of the exclusions reinforces the ordinary‑meaning distinction. Excluding NFTs, promos, and point systems signals the market’s focus on a token “launched by Polymarket” in the way that crypto users commonly understand—a token product held/used beyond mere internal accounting. By contrast, the record’s “pUSD — CollateralToken (proxy)” is a USDC‑backed wrapper minted/burned to serve exchange collateralization. Treating any ERC‑20 used internally as an “official token launch” would collapse the resolution’s distinction and sweep in routine contract refactors.

Fallback even under plaintiff’s broader theory
If the Council were inclined to treat any ERC‑20 named in Polymarket docs as an “official token,” the record still fails to uniquely and sufficiently prove: (a) deployer/owner linkage to Polymarket, (b) immutable, pre‑deadline publication of the claimed announcements, and (c) wallet‑level transferability in practice rather than assertion. The plaintiff asks you to bridge each of these with inference.

Record/argument separation
— Record facts: PX‑1/PX‑2 report that Polymarket docs used the words “collateral token (pUSD),” list a contracts address, and show dated changelog entries in April 2026. PX‑1 admits the captures are post‑run preservations and that archival/timestamp evidence is missing.
— Our inferences: From that thin record, it is at least equally plausible that pUSD is an internal wrapper token whose introduction was a technical migration, not an “official token launch” as contemplated by the proposition.

Disposition
On this record, ambiguity persists on the core element—whether Polymarket “launched an official fungible token.” The plaintiff’s evidence depends on editable documentation, lacks immutable timestamps and issuer attribution, and frames a collateral‑contract migration as a token launch. Under a preponderance standard, that does not tip the scales. The Council should find the proposition not proven and answer NO.

### Surrebuttals

[defendant] Council members,

Plaintiff’s rebuttal turns on two moves: (1) redefining “official token launch” to mean “any ERC‑20 Polymarket uses,” and (2) elevating editable docs to dispositive proof of both “official release” and “public communication of a launch.” On this record, neither move tips the scale under a preponderance standard.

1) Collateral ≠ “official token launch” on this record
• The proposition’s elements are affirmative: Polymarket must have launched a token and publicly communicated that launch. The exclusions only say what does not count; they do not transform every non‑excluded instrument into a launch. 
• PX‑1/PX‑2 consistently describe pUSD as “the collateral token” introduced within a coordinated infrastructure upgrade (CLOB V2). That is internal plumbing, not a standalone token product launched to holders as “the Polymarket token.” The cited materials never quote Polymarket saying “we launched a token” or “this is our official token.” They say the exchange now uses a new collateral token.

2) “Officially released by Polymarket” is not established by an address listing
• The “Contracts” page mapping “pUSD — CollateralToken (proxy)” to a Polygon address shows integration and nomenclature. It is not proof of issuance or a token launch. The record has no deployer/owner linkage, no statement of supply control, and no communication that Polymarket is issuing a token to the public. 
• The same pages say pUSD is “backed by USDC,” reinforcing that it functions as a wrapped/settlement instrument for trading, not a launched token in the ordinary sense contemplated by the proposition.

3) “Publicly communicated launch” is missing
• The changelog entries (Apr 17, 2026 pre‑announcement; Apr 28, 2026 go‑live ≈ Apr 28, 2026 04:00 PDT) describe a platform upgrade that includes “a new collateral token (pUSD).” That is a component note in a technical release log, not a public launch announcement of a token. The proposition requires Polymarket to have publicly communicated the token’s launch, not merely documented an internal contract swap after shipping.

4) Timing does not cure the categorical gap
• Even accepting plaintiff’s dates and PST normalization, the question is not when an upgrade happened but whether Polymarket launched a token and said so. On this record, the most that is shown is an infrastructure migration that uses an ERC‑20 as collateral. That is equally—if not more—consistent with “no token launch” under the proposition’s ordinary meaning.

5) This is about weight, not hyper‑formal proof
• We are not demanding cryptographic signatures. We are asking for clear language from Polymarket that the thing purportedly “launched” is a token launch at all. The only Polymarket words in evidence—“collateral token,” “single source of truth” for addresses, “CLOB V2 is live”—support the infrastructure reading as much as the plaintiff’s.

Bottom line
• Accepted record facts: pUSD is described in Polymarket docs as a standard ERC‑20 used as collateral; a contracts page lists its address; a changelog on Apr 17/28, 2026 references the upgrade. 
• Missing for the proposition: an explicit Polymarket statement that it “launched” a token as such or that pUSD is Polymarket’s “official token,” plus credible issuer linkage beyond an address label in docs.

If two readings are in equipoise, the moving party has not carried the burden. On this record, “official token launch” remains at least equally plausibly unproven. The Council should answer NO.
