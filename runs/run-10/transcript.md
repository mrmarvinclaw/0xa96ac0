### Openings

#### Plaintiff Opening

Council Members,

Dispute and thesis. The proposition asks whether, before January 1, 2027 at 00:00 PST, Polymarket launched an official fungible token—meaning a token that is (a) fungible, (b) officially released by Polymarket, and (c) publicly communicated through Polymarket’s own official channels—while expressly excluding NFTs, promotional giveaways, tokenized points, and launches done in collaboration with other projects or communities. Our theory is that Polymarket did in fact launch such a token within the stated window. The Council’s task is to weigh whether that is more likely than not (preponderance of the evidence).

What really decides this case. The outcome turns on five linked factual determinations:
1) Token existence and type: Identification of a specific on-chain asset that is plainly fungible (e.g., adherence to a fungible token interface and non-unique units), not an NFT and not a mere points ledger.
2) Official release by Polymarket: Evidence that Polymarket authorized and executed the launch—e.g., deployment from a Polymarket-controlled account or an explicit attribution of responsibility to Polymarket, as opposed to a community or partner.
3) Public communication via official channels: A contemporaneous, outward-facing announcement from Polymarket’s verified or official outlets (e.g., website, blog, documentation, product UI notices, corporate press room, or verified social media accounts), rather than third‑party reporting or private communications.
4) Timing: Reliable timestamps showing the public communication and/or on-chain launch occurred before 00:00 PST on January 1, 2027 (including appropriate time‑zone conversion and chain timestamp verification).
5) Exclusions: Clear differentiation that the asset is not (i) an NFT collection, (ii) a promotional coupon or giveaway instrument with no persistent token identity, (iii) a tokenized points/rewards system, or (iv) a launch primarily attributable to another project or community (“collaboration”) where Polymarket is not the launching principal.

Proof that satisfies the burden. Under a preponderance standard, the following would be sufficient when taken together:
- A Polymarket-issued statement on an official channel announcing the token’s launch, name/symbol, and intended role; and
- On-chain evidence tying the token’s deployment or control to a Polymarket-owned or Polymarket‑designated address, with block/time data anchoring it before the deadline; and
- Materials clarifying that the token is a standalone Polymarket issuance (not points, not an NFT, not a co‑branded partner token), such as documentation or policy pages that distinguish it from rewards or promotional instruments.

What would defeat the proposition. The proposition fails if the record shows only:
- Third‑party or community‑issued tokens using Polymarket’s name without Polymarket’s authorization;
- Internal or promotional “points,” off‑chain credits, or time‑limited rewards that never constituted a fungible token on a public network;
- NFT collections or soul‑bound assets; or
- A token that Polymarket co‑launched principally under another project’s umbrella, or any qualifying token whose launch or official communication post‑dates 00:00 PST on January 1, 2027.

Evidence the Council should expect to see. To resolve these questions, the most probative materials are:
- Official Polymarket communications: website/blog posts, documentation pages, press releases, product release notes, or messages from verified corporate social accounts explicitly describing the token and its launch.
- On‑chain records: contract addresses, deployer/owner roles, verified source code, and block/time data; where possible, cryptographic or operational ties to Polymarket (e.g., multisig ownership, known admin keys).
- Product/UI evidence: in‑app notices or documentation integrating the token for fees, utility, or governance.
- Archival corroboration: independent web archives preserving the announcements and timestamps.
- Policies/terms: distinctions between tokens and non‑token rewards, clarifying the exclusions.

Plan of proof. In subsequent filings, we will assemble an integrated timeline aligning (i) official announcements with (ii) on‑chain deployment data and (iii) documentation that distinguishes the token from excluded categories. We will also address time‑zone normalization to PST and authenticate the provenance of the communications and addresses. If needed, we will obtain expert support on on‑chain attribution.

Requested direction. The Council should evaluate the forthcoming record against the five elements above. If our evidence shows an official Polymarket announcement and attributable on‑chain fungible token before the stated deadline—and the exclusions do not apply—the proposition is more likely than not true and should be sustained.

#### Defendant Opening

Council Members,

Dispute and theory. The question is precise: Before 00:00 PST on January 1, 2027, did Polymarket launch an official fungible token and publicly communicate that launch on Polymarket’s own channels, while excluding NFTs, promotional items, points, and partner‑led tokens? Our theory is that this proposition is not proven. Under a preponderance standard, ambiguity in issuer identity, on‑chain control, or first‑party communications should defeat the claim.

Determinative issues. Three definitions will control the outcome:
1) Official fungible token. A public‑chain fungible asset (standard token interface; interchangeable units) for which Polymarket is issuer of record. That requires: (a) on‑chain deployment from, or post‑deployment control by, a Polymarket‑controlled address; and (b) Polymarket’s own description of the asset as its token product—not merely a token it integrates or that a partner/community issued.
2) Launched. Initial mainnet creation and public availability for users, not testnets, internal pilots, code/UI placeholders, or later third‑party wrappers/bridges. Supporting or listing someone else’s token is not a Polymarket launch.
3) Publicly communicated through official channels. Announcement on Polymarket’s verified outlets (website, product blog/notes, docs, in‑app notices, or verified corporate social accounts). Media reports, exchange listings, partner blogs, investor or employee personal posts, and leaks do not satisfy this without matching first‑party communication.

Timing. The deadline equals 08:00 UTC on January 1, 2027. Both (i) the on‑chain launch and (ii) an official‑channel communication must predate that moment. Any timestamps should be normalized to PST and corroborated.

What would satisfy the burden. The proponent should produce, in combination:
- A first‑party Polymarket communication clearly stating it launched its own fungible token, naming it and describing intended role; and
- On‑chain artifacts tying the token to Polymarket at genesis: deployer/owner/admin roles attributable to Polymarket, or equivalent control evidence; and
- Materials confirming the asset is not an NFT, not a promotional coupon/giveaway, not a points ledger, and not a partner‑led issuance where Polymarket is secondary.

What would not suffice. The claim fails if the record consists of: (a) third‑party tokens using the Polymarket name without first‑party attribution; (b) rewards/points or off‑chain credits later labeled as “tokens”; (c) UI references, code comments, or roadmaps without a production mainnet launch; (d) partner/exchange announcements without a parallel Polymarket announcement inside the window; (e) retrospective statements dated after the deadline; or (f) ambiguous on‑chain deployments without a reliable link to Polymarket control.

Evidence the Council should seek. Most probative are: (1) Polymarket’s official posts, product notes, docs, terms, or verified social messages contemporaneously identifying the token as launched; (2) contract addresses with deployer/owner roles demonstrably tied to Polymarket (including any multisig/admin attribution); (3) product/UI records integrating the token in production; and (4) independent web archives/time‑stamped captures fixing when any official statements appeared. Absence of first‑party attribution or on‑chain control should be treated as dispositive against the proposition.

Defense plan. We will test any candidate asset against these elements, align on‑chain provenance and control, normalize times to PST, and review official channels for contemporaneous first‑party communications. Where evidence points to partner‑led, post‑window, or points/NFT‑adjacent activity, we will show why the exclusions apply. We will also identify any first‑party materials that distinguish tokens from points to confirm non‑qualification.

Requested direction. Hold the proponent to clear, contemporaneous, first‑party evidence plus attributable on‑chain control before the deadline. If such proof is incomplete or equivocal, the proposition is not more likely than not and should be rejected.

#### Defendant Argument

Council Members,

Position and burden. The proposition requires proof, by a preponderance of the evidence, that before 00:00 PST on January 1, 2027, Polymarket (i) launched a fungible token, (ii) officially released by Polymarket, and (iii) publicly communicated the launch on Polymarket’s own channels. NFTs, promos, points, and partner‑led launches do not qualify. On the present record, that showing has not been made.

What the record actually contains. The plaintiff’s case rests on four Polymarket documentation pages and a Polygonscan view, all preserved after-the-fact (PX‑1, PX‑2). Those pages describe a “new collateral token (pUSD)” and list a contract address on a “Contracts” page characterized as the “single source of truth.” The captures themselves were created post‑arbitration; the record expressly acknowledges that server‑side publication timestamps and independent archival snapshots were not obtained. There is no on‑chain provenance tying the deployer/owner/admin of the token contract to a Polymarket‑controlled address, and no cryptographic or archival attestation of when the relevant pages were first published.

Three dispositive defects prevent the plaintiff from carrying its burden:

1) Issuer‑of‑record is not proven. To satisfy “officially released by Polymarket,” there should be reliable indicia that Polymarket is the token’s issuer at genesis or exercises post‑deployment control (e.g., deployer or proxy admin owned by a Polymarket multisig, signed ownership/roles, or equivalent). The record provides none. A name on a documentation page and a contract listing—without any on‑chain attribution of deployer/admin to Polymarket—does not establish issuance. Integrations routinely list third‑party tokens; listing alone is not “launch.”

2) “Launch” versus internal plumbing. The cited materials describe pUSD as a “collateral token” introduced as part of an exchange upgrade. Even accepting every word at face value, that is a technical component of the trading system. The proposition, however, asks whether Polymarket “launched an official fungible token.” Nothing in the record shows (a) a standalone token launch announcement, (b) distribution, minting policy, or (c) a statement that Polymarket is issuing a Polymarket‑branded token as a product in its own right. Calling a standard ERC‑20 used internally for collateral “our launch of an official token” stretches the term beyond its ordinary meaning and beyond what the market text contemplates, which carefully excludes non‑token reward systems and collaborations but does not transform an internal accounting wrapper into an “official token launch.”

3) Timing is not independently authenticated. The plaintiff relies on doc‑site dates (e.g., “Apr 17, 2026” and “Apr 28, 2026”). Yet PX‑1 concedes these captures were made after the open‑record run and lacks independent time anchoring (no Wayback/archival hash, no repo commit history, no signed release artifacts). In a proceeding where the decisive question is whether the communication and the alleged launch occurred before 00:00 PST on 2027‑01‑01, mutable web pages without third‑party timestamps are insufficient to carry the day. Preponderance requires more than “the page currently says it was April 2026.”

Exclusions and collaboration. The record also leaves unresolved whether pUSD is a wrapper/derivative reliant on third‑party issuance (backed by USDC and deployed on Polygon). If pUSD depends on a partner’s mint/burn rails or custody, the “official launch” may be collaborative in substance, which the market text excludes. The plaintiff offers no details on custody, minting authority, or counterparties that would dispel that ambiguity.

Why Polygonscan labeling and a “single source of truth” page are not enough. Explorer labels are crowd‑editable and not first‑party. The “Contracts” page language is self‑referential and untimestamped. Neither proves issuer identity or publication timing, and neither substitutes for on‑chain control evidence.

What adequate proof would have looked like here. To satisfy this market’s elements, the proponent should have produced, in combination: (a) an independently timestamped first‑party announcement contemporaneous with the alleged launch; (b) on‑chain deployer/owner/admin roles cryptographically attributable to Polymarket; and (c) documentation clarifying minting policy and confirming the token was not a partner‑led wrapper. None of that appears in this record.

Bottom line. Even crediting that pUSD exists and is fungible, the proponent has not shown that Polymarket launched it as Polymarket’s own official token—nor that any qualifying launch and official communication indisputably occurred before the deadline. The issuer‑of‑record, the nature of the act (“launch” versus internal collateral), and the timing remain insufficiently proven. Under a preponderance standard, those unresolved gaps mean the scales do not tip in favor of the proposition.

Requested ruling. Reject the proposition for failure of proof.

### Rebuttals

#### Plaintiff Rebuttal

Council Members,

The defense’s objections ask you to import requirements the market text does not contain and to discount multiple, mutually‑reinforcing first‑party records. Under a preponderance standard, that is a bridge too far.

1) “Officially released by Polymarket” does not mean cryptographic issuer‑of‑record proof. The market requires an official Polymarket release and public communication on official channels—not that the deployer key be proven to belong to a Polymarket multisig. Here, Polymarket’s own docs (PX‑1, PX‑2) (a) name and describe “Polymarket USD (pUSD)” as its collateral token, (b) list the token on the “single source of truth” Contracts page as “pUSD — CollateralToken (proxy)” at a specified Polygon address, and (c) integrate it into a production upgrade. That is an official release by Polymarket, publicly communicated, regardless of whether the record also traces the proxy admin on‑chain. Demanding deployer/admin linkage elevates the burden beyond the market’s language.

2) “Launch” is satisfied. The defense recasts pUSD as mere “internal plumbing.” But Polymarket’s materials repeatedly characterize pUSD as a new, standard ERC‑20 collateral token used “for all trading on Polymarket,” shipped as part of CLOB V2. Shipping a new ERC‑20 that users must hold and transfer to trade is the paradigmatic act of launching a token. The market text nowhere limits “official token” to a standalone, airdropped, or governance‑branded asset. It excludes NFTs, promos, and points; pUSD is none of those.

3) Timing is adequately proven for preponderance. Three independent first‑party pages—the changelog, the migration guide, and the concept page—cross‑reference one another and place announcement on April 17, 2026 and production cutover on April 28, 2026, with time normalization already provided in the technical report. The defense points to the absence of third‑party archives but offers no contrary evidence. On this record, consistent, detailed Polymarket‑hosted timestamps across multiple pages are more likely than not accurate—and they predate the January 1, 2027 00:00 PST deadline by months.

4) Collaboration exclusion does not apply. That pUSD is backed by USDC and deployed on Polygon reflects infrastructure choices, not a joint launch “with other projects or communities.” The name, branding, contracts listing, and communications are first‑party Polymarket. There is no partner claim of launch.

5) Explorer/attribution. We did not rest on Polygonscan labels. The workhorse attribution is the Contracts page, which Polymarket itself designates as authoritative. That is exactly the kind of official‑channel communication the market requires.

Bottom line. The plaintiff’s evidence checks every element the market actually specifies: fungibility (ERC‑20), official Polymarket release, public communication on official channels, timing before the deadline, and inapplicability of exclusions (PX‑1, PX‑2). The defense invites heightened proof (on‑chain control attestation, external archives) that the market did not demand. On the standard that governs here—more likely than not—the scales favor sustaining the proposition.

Requested ruling: Sustain.

### Surrebuttals

#### Defendant Surrebuttal

Council Members,

The plaintiff’s rebuttal turns on three claims: (1) Polymarket’s own docs suffice to prove “officially released by Polymarket”; (2) shipping a collateral ERC‑20 inside a backend upgrade equals a public token “launch”; and (3) cross‑referenced dates on the same site are adequate to fix timing. On this record, none of those claims closes the dispositive gaps.

1) “Officially released by Polymarket.” The market text requires Polymarket to be the releasing principal. PX‑1/PX‑2 show only that Polymarket’s docs describe pUSD and that a “Contracts” page lists an address while calling itself a “single source of truth.” They do not show issuer‑of‑record facts: no deployer/owner/admin linkage to a Polymarket multisig, no signed attribution, no statement that Polymarket minted/controls supply. Even accepting the pages at face value, that evidence is equally consistent with Polymarket integrating or wrapping a third‑party stable asset for collateral. Asking for some reliable indicator of issuing agency is not heightening the burden; it is what distinguishes “we launched our token” from “we use this token.” Branding alone (“Polymarket USD”) does not prove issuance.

2) “Launch.” The cited texts describe a coordinated infrastructure upgrade that “includes a new collateral token (pUSD).” They do not announce a standalone token launch as a product in its own right (no distribution policy, no launch announcement separate from the upgrade, no supply/minting details). Even crediting that pUSD is fungible and used “for all trading,” that remains internal plumbing unless the record shows Polymarket launched the token as Polymarket’s token product. The proposition asks about an “official token” launch; nothing in PX‑1/PX‑2 shows Polymarket characterizing this as such, rather than documenting a component of CLOB V2.

3) Timing. The only timestamps are on Polymarket‑hosted pages captured after the run. PX‑1 itself concedes the lack of independent archival snapshots, server‑side publication proofs, or repo commit anchors. Interlocking dates on the same mutable domain are not external corroboration. Where the dispositive element is “before 00:00 PST on 2027‑01‑01,” at least one independent, contemporaneous anchor is needed to tip the scales. None appears in this record. Time‑zone normalization cannot cure the absence of reliable time anchoring.

Exclusions/partner ambiguity. PX‑1/PX‑2 also describe pUSD as “backed by USDC” and deployed on Polygon. That architecture is at least consistent with a wrapper or custody dependency. Whether or not it ultimately triggers the “collaboration” exclusion, the proponent has not dispelled ambiguity about who actually issues and controls the token—ambiguity that matters to whether Polymarket “launched” it.

Even accepting the plaintiff’s core factual premises (pUSD exists; the docs say what they say), the record still does not show that Polymarket launched an official fungible token and publicly communicated that launch before the deadline. The issuer‑of‑record, the nature of the act (launch vs. internal collateral), and the timing remain insufficiently proven. Under a preponderance standard, unresolved material ambiguity means the scales do not tip for the proposition.

Requested ruling: Reject the proposition for failure of proof.

#### Defendant Closing

Council Members,

The proposition requires proof, by a preponderance of the evidence, that before 00:00 PST on January 1, 2027, Polymarket (1) launched a fungible token, (2) that token was officially released by Polymarket, and (3) Polymarket publicly communicated that launch on its own channels. NFT, promo, points, and partner‑led launches are excluded. On this record, the proponent has not carried that burden.

What is fairly established. The record contains Polymarket‑hosted documentation pages that describe something called “Polymarket USD (pUSD)” as a collateral ERC‑20 on Polygon and a Contracts page that lists a pUSD address. There is also a capture of a Polygonscan page. PX‑1; PX‑2. That supports the existence of a fungible ERC‑20 used in Polymarket’s system.

Where the proof falls short on the dispositive elements:

1) Officially released by Polymarket (releasing principal). The market text requires that Polymarket be the actor that released the token—i.e., that this is Polymarket’s own token, not merely a token it uses or integrates. The exhibits do not tie deployer/owner/admin to a Polymarket‑controlled account, do not include any signed attribution, and do not contain a first‑party statement that Polymarket issued or controls pUSD’s supply. They show a documentation description and a self‑labeled “single source of truth” listing. Those facts are equally consistent with integration of a third‑party or wrapper token. Under a preponderance standard, when the evidence is in equipoise between “we launched it” and “we use it,” the proponent has not met its burden.

2) Launch versus internal plumbing. The cited texts describe a coordinated infrastructure upgrade that “includes a new collateral token (pUSD)” used “for all trading.” PX‑1; PX‑2. Nothing in the record shows a standalone launch of a Polymarket token as a product in its own right—no distribution or minting policy, no explanation of issuance authority, no separate token‑launch announcement. Characterizing a new internal collateral wrapper as an “official token launch” stretches beyond what these materials say. The proposition asks about Polymarket launching an official fungible token; the record reads as Polymarket documenting a component of CLOB V2.

3) Timing before the deadline. The only specific dates (April 17 and April 28, 2026) appear on Polymarket‑hosted pages captured after the run. PX‑1 candidly acknowledges the absence of independent archival timestamps, server‑side publication proofs, repository commit anchors, or on‑chain time anchoring in the record. The Polygonscan capture is cited only as corroboration and the record does not extract or rely on a creation timestamp from it. Where the dispositive question is “before 00:00 PST on 2027‑01‑01,” internal web dates without third‑party anchoring are not enough to tip the scale.

4) Exclusions and collaboration ambiguity. PX‑1/PX‑2 describe pUSD as “backed by USDC” and deployed on Polygon. That architecture is at least consistent with a wrapper or custody dependency. Whether or not that ultimately triggers the “collaboration” exclusion, the proponent has not dispelled who actually issues and controls the asset—ambiguity that bears directly on whether Polymarket “launched” it.

Addressing the plaintiff’s core rejoinders. The plaintiff argues that requiring on‑chain control evidence “imports” a higher standard than the market text. Not so. The defense is not insisting on cryptographic proof in all cases; we are pointing out that nothing in this record reliably identifies Polymarket as the releasing principal. Some combination of a clear first‑party launch statement (“we launched/issued pUSD”) or attributable on‑chain roles would suffice. The plaintiff produced neither. They further argue that cross‑referenced dates on three pages should satisfy timing under preponderance. But those pages are all on the same mutable domain and were preserved post‑hoc; the record lacks any independent anchoring. In a market that turns on “before the deadline,” that gap is decisive.

Bottom line. The record shows a fungible token exists and that Polymarket describes and uses it as collateral. It does not show, more likely than not, that Polymarket launched that token as its own official issuance, nor does it independently fix that any qualifying launch and official communication occurred before 00:00 PST on January 1, 2027. With issuer identity, the nature of the act (launch vs. integration), and timing all insufficiently proven, the scales do not tip in favor of the proposition.

Requested ruling: Reject the proposition for failure of proof under the preponderance standard.
